## 3. Pre-Work Plan

**Prior Knowledge Activation:**
- In Module 5.1, you learned about reduced visibility. Many weather conditions also reduce visibility. How do rain and fog compound driving risks beyond just visibility?
- In Module 2.2, you learned about traction. How do wet and icy roads change traction?

**Pre-Reading/Viewing:**
- Idaho Driver's Manual, section on Adverse Weather Driving (~8 pages)
- Guiding questions: What is hydroplaning and how do you recover? Why are bridges and overpasses dangerous in cold weather?

**Readiness Self-Check:**
1. What is black ice?
2. What should you do if your car starts to hydroplane?
3. Why do bridges freeze before regular road surfaces?
4. Should you use high beams in fog?

**Key Terms:**
1. Hydroplaning
2. Black ice
3. Traction
4. Tire chains
5. Crosswind
6. Fog lights
7. Emergency kit
8. Winter driving
9. Bridge freezing

## 5. Activity Plan

### Activity 1: Weather Condition Risk Analysis

**Type:** Practice
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-5.2.1
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. For each of 5 weather conditions (rain, snow, ice, fog, wind), identify:
   - The primary driving risk
   - The effect on traction and visibility
   - At least two compensation strategies
   - The appropriate speed and following distance adjustment
2. Compare to the answer key.

**Deliverable:** Completed weather risk analysis chart

### Activity 2: Traction Emergency Scenarios

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-5.2.2
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Read 5 traction emergency scenarios (hydroplaning on highway, encountering black ice on a bridge, driving into dense fog, crossing a wind-exposed overpass, first rain on oily surface).
2. For each, describe the step-by-step recovery/response procedure.
3. Identify what the driver could have done to prevent the situation.
4. Check against model answers.

**Deliverable:** Written emergency responses and prevention strategies

### Activity 3: Winter Preparedness Discussion

**Type:** Interaction
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Learner
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-5.2.3
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** Yes (5% of module grade)

**Instructions:**
1. Create your personal winter driving preparedness plan for Idaho, including:
   - Emergency kit contents (minimum 8 items with justification)
   - Three pre-trip preparation steps
   - Your decision criteria for when NOT to drive
2. Post your plan and respond to one classmate — suggest items or criteria they may have missed.

**Deliverable:** Discussion post (minimum 100 words) + 1 response (minimum 30 words)

## 7. Reflection & Synthesis Plan

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Each weather condition presents unique risks — rain causes hydroplaning, ice reduces traction to near-zero, fog eliminates visual lead time, wind destabilizes vehicles.
2. The universal adverse weather strategies are: reduce speed, increase following distance, use gentle inputs, and use appropriate lights.
3. Idaho winters demand specific preparation — an emergency kit, proper tires, and a willingness to delay trips can save your life.
4. Knowing when NOT to drive is the most important adverse weather skill.

**Connection to Next Module:**
Module 5.3 (Responding to Emergencies) covers what to do when things go wrong despite preparation — vehicle failures, skids, and off-road recovery.

**Self-Reflection Prompt:**
1. Which Idaho weather condition concerns you most as a new driver? What will you do to prepare?
2. How does this module connect to what you learned about traction in Module 2.2?
3. What three items would you put in your car's emergency kit first?
