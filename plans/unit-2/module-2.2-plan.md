## 3. Pre-Work Plan

**Prior Knowledge Activation:**
- You learned about braking in Module 1.2. Now think: why does it take longer to stop when going faster?
- Have you ever felt yourself lean sideways in a car going around a curve? What force causes that?

**Pre-Reading/Viewing:**
- Idaho Driver's Manual, section on Natural Laws and Stopping Distance (~10 pages)
- Guiding questions: What happens to kinetic energy when you double your speed? What three components make up total stopping distance?

**Readiness Self-Check:**
1. Can you define gravity?
2. What do you think happens to a car's energy when its speed doubles?
3. Do you know what friction is?
4. Can you name one factor that affects how quickly a car stops?

**Key Terms:**
1. Kinetic energy
2. Gravity
3. Friction
4. Traction
5. Centrifugal force
6. Perception distance
7. Reaction distance
8. Braking distance
9. Total stopping distance
10. Vehicle balance

## 5. Activity Plan

### Activity 1: Natural Laws Cause-Effect Mapping

**Type:** Practice
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-2.2.1, MLO-2.2.2
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Given 10 cause statements (e.g., "Speed doubles," "Road is wet," "Driver brakes hard in a turn"), match each to its correct effect.
2. Write a one-sentence explanation for each match.
3. Compare to the answer key.

**Deliverable:** Completed cause-effect chart with explanations

### Activity 2: Stopping Distance Calculator

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-2.2.4
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Using the stopping distance formula and provided data tables, calculate total stopping distance for:
   - 25 mph on dry pavement
   - 45 mph on dry pavement
   - 45 mph on wet pavement
   - 65 mph on dry pavement
2. Answer: How many car lengths is each distance?
3. Explain why the 45 mph wet calculation differs from the 45 mph dry calculation.

**Deliverable:** Four calculations with car-length conversions and written explanation

### Activity 3: Hill and Curve Navigation Discussion

**Type:** Interaction
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Learner
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-2.2.3
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** Yes (5% of module grade)

**Instructions:**
1. View the hill-and-curve scenario diagram posted by the instructor.
2. Describe the correct approach speed, lane position at each phase (approach, apex, exit), and any special precautions.
3. Respond to one classmate's post with additional observations or corrections.

**Deliverable:** Discussion post (minimum 75 words) + 1 response (minimum 30 words)

## 7. Reflection & Synthesis Plan

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Doubling your speed quadruples kinetic energy — speed is the single biggest factor in crash severity.
2. Traction is limited and shared among steering, braking, and acceleration — aggressive inputs in any one area reduce available traction for the others.
3. Total stopping distance = perception + reaction + braking distance — all three increase with speed, and conditions can multiply each.
4. On hills and curves, slow before the turn, not in it — and always adjust for visibility and traction.

**Connection to Next Module:**
Module 2.3 (Managing Visibility) will teach you how to use your vision effectively while driving — because seeing hazards early is your best tool for applying the physics principles you learned here.

**Self-Reflection Prompt:**
1. How does understanding kinetic energy change the way you think about speeding?
2. Which stopping distance factor surprised you most, and why?
3. How will knowledge of vehicle balance help you when you practice driving on hills and curves?
