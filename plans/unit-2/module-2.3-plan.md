## 3. Pre-Work Plan

**Prior Knowledge Activation:**
- In Module 2.2, you learned about stopping distance and reaction time. Now think: how does what you see (and when you see it) affect your ability to react?
- Try this: focus on one word on this page. Without moving your eyes, what can you see to the left and right? That is your peripheral vision.

**Pre-Reading/Viewing:**
- Idaho Driver's Manual, section on Vision and Driving (~6 pages)
- Guiding questions: What is the difference between central and peripheral vision? What does "aim high in steering" mean?

**Readiness Self-Check:**
1. Can you estimate how many degrees of vision you have straight ahead?
2. What do you think happens to your field of vision as speed increases?
3. Have you heard of the Smith System?
4. What is an optical illusion?

**Key Terms:**
1. Central vision
2. Peripheral vision
3. Visual lead time
4. Tunnel vision effect
5. Aim high in steering
6. Keep eyes moving
7. Get the big picture
8. Leave yourself an out
9. Selective search pattern
10. Optical illusion

## 4. Core Instruction Plan

**Primary Modalities:** Text + Visual diagrams + Video

**Content Chunks:**

| Chunk | Topic | Duration | Engagement Prompt |
|-------|-------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | Central Vision (3 degrees) and Peripheral Vision (up to 180 degrees) | 8 min | "Why is it dangerous to stare at one thing while driving?" |
| 2 | How Speed Reduces Peripheral Vision | 7 min | "At 60 mph, your peripheral vision narrows significantly. How does this affect your ability to spot hazards?" |
| 3 | Smith System: Aim High in Steering and Keep Eyes Moving | 8 min | "What does 'aim high' actually mean in practice? How far ahead should you look?" |
| 4 | Smith System: Get the Big Picture and Leave Yourself an Out | 8 min | "Describe a situation where having an 'out' could save you from a crash." |
| 5 | Smith System: Make Sure They See You | 7 min | "What are three ways to make sure other drivers see you?" |
| 6 | Vehicle Control Sequence: See-Think-Do | 7 min | "Why is vision the first step in the control sequence?" |
| 7 | Optical Illusions and Their Dangers | 7 min | "How might a wet road surface trick you into misjudging distance?" |

**Worked Examples:**

## 5. Activity Plan

### Activity 1: Fields of Vision Diagram Exercise

**Type:** Practice
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-2.3.1
**Estimated Time:** 10 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Label the vision diagram with central and peripheral vision zones.
2. Write the degree range for each.
3. Give two examples of how each type of vision is used while driving.
4. Check your answers against the key.

**Deliverable:** Labeled vision diagram with examples

### Activity 2: Speed and Vision Analysis

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-2.3.2
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Review the 6 driving scenarios at different speeds (20, 35, 45, 55, 65, 75 mph).
2. For each, predict: (a) approximate peripheral vision narrowing, (b) visual lead time needed, (c) hazard detection difficulty.
3. Rank the scenarios from lowest to highest risk and explain your ranking.

**Deliverable:** Completed analysis chart with risk ranking

### Activity 3: Smith System Application Scenarios

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-2.3.3
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** Yes (5% of module grade)

**Instructions:**
1. Review 5 driving scenario images (city intersection, highway merge, school zone, parking lot, rural curve).
2. For each image, describe specifically how you would apply all five Smith System techniques.
3. Submit your responses.

**Deliverable:** Written application of all five techniques to each of the 5 scenarios

## 6. Assessment Plan

### Assessment: Module 2.3 Stop and Check Quiz

**Type:** Quiz
**MLO(s) Assessed:** MLO-2.3.1, MLO-2.3.2, MLO-2.3.3
**Weight:** 10% of module grade
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Attempts:** 2 allowed

**Description:**
Combined quiz on vision types, speed effects on vision, and Smith System application.

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Vision Types (MLO-2.3.1) | Correctly explains both types with degree ranges and driving applications | Explains both types; minor detail missing | Explains one type correctly; second is vague | Cannot distinguish central from peripheral vision |
| Speed-Vision Relationship (MLO-2.3.2) | Accurately analyzes speed effects on vision in 90%+ of scenarios | Analyzes most correctly; minor error | Analyzes some; key relationship missed | Cannot connect speed to vision changes |

**Academic Integrity Notice:**
Complete individually. Notes and manual may be referenced. Do not share answers. AI tool use is not permitted.

## 7. Reflection & Synthesis Plan

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Central vision is sharp but narrow (3 degrees) — you must constantly move your eyes to scan your environment effectively.
2. Speed reduces peripheral vision, creating a "tunnel vision" effect that limits hazard detection.
3. The five Smith System techniques (aim high, keep eyes moving, get the big picture, leave yourself an out, make sure they see you) form a complete visual strategy.
4. Vision leads every driving decision — the see-think-do sequence begins with your eyes.

**Connection to Next Module:**
Module 2.4 (Space Management) builds on visibility by teaching you how to manage the space around your vehicle — what you see determines how you manage your zones.

**Self-Reflection Prompt:**
1. Which Smith System technique do you think will be hardest to practice consistently?
2. How does understanding tunnel vision change the way you think about highway driving?
3. Have you ever experienced an optical illusion while in a moving vehicle? What happened?
