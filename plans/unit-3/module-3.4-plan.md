## 3. Pre-Work Plan

**Prior Knowledge Activation:**
- Idaho has vast rural areas. What do you think makes driving on a two-lane rural highway different from driving in town?
- Have you ever seen a "slow-moving vehicle" triangle on farm equipment? What does it mean for the driver behind it?

**Pre-Reading/Viewing:**
- Idaho Driver's Manual, sections on Rural Driving and Railroad Crossings (~8 pages)
- Guiding questions: What is Idaho's Open Range Law? What must you do at a railroad crossing with flashing lights?

**Readiness Self-Check:**
1. Are speed limits generally higher or lower on rural roads?
2. What should you do if a deer is standing in the road?
3. What does the orange triangle on the back of a vehicle mean?
4. Can you cross railroad tracks when the gates are coming down?

**Key Terms:**
1. Rural driving
2. Open range
3. Open Range Law
4. Slow-moving vehicle emblem
5. Railroad grade crossing
6. Controlled crossing
7. Uncontrolled crossing
8. Crossbuck sign
9. Loose gravel
10. Limited access

## 4. Core Instruction Plan

**Primary Modalities:** Text + Visual (photos/diagrams) + Video

**Content Chunks:**

| Chunk | Topic | Duration | Engagement Prompt |
|-------|-------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | Rural Driving Characteristics: Speed, Width, Surfaces | 8 min | "Why are rural roads more dangerous per mile driven than urban roads?" |
| 2 | Rural Hazards: Curves, Hills, Visibility | 8 min | "How should you adjust your driving on a narrow rural road with blind hills?" |
| 3 | Rural Signs, Signals, and Markings | 7 min | "What rural-specific signs should you watch for that you wouldn't see in a city?" |
| 4 | Animals: Wildlife and Livestock | 8 min | "If a deer jumps in front of your car, should you swerve or brake? Why?" |

## 5. Activity Plan

### Activity 1: Rural Hazard Identification

**Type:** Practice
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-3.4.1
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Review 8 rural driving images.
2. For each, identify all hazards present.
3. Describe the driving adjustment required for each hazard.
4. Compare to the answer key.

**Deliverable:** Hazard identification chart with driving adjustments

### Activity 2: Open Range Law Case Study

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-3.4.2
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Read 4 short scenarios involving animal encounters on Idaho roads.
2. For each, determine: (a) Is this an open range area? (b) What is the correct driver response? (c) Who bears liability? (d) What could the driver have done to prevent the situation?
3. Compare to model answers.

**Deliverable:** Written case study responses

### Activity 3: Railroad Crossing Procedure Activity

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-3.4.3
**Estimated Time:** 10 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Review 6 railroad crossing scenarios (controlled with gates, controlled with lights only, uncontrolled, stalled vehicle on tracks, train visible, no train visible).
2. For each, describe the correct procedure step by step.
3. Check your answers.

**Deliverable:** Completed crossing procedure worksheet

## 6. Assessment Plan

### Assessment: Module 3.4 Stop and Check Quizzes

**Type:** Quiz (2 quizzes)
**MLO(s) Assessed:** MLO-3.4.1, MLO-3.4.2, MLO-3.4.3
**Weight:** 10% of module grade
**Duration:** 15 minutes total
**Attempts:** 2 allowed

**Description:**
Quiz 1 covers rural characteristics, hazards, and railroad crossings. Quiz 2 covers animal encounters and Open Range Law.

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Rural Hazards (MLO-3.4.1) | Identifies 6+ characteristics/hazards with correct adjustments | Identifies 4–5 with mostly correct adjustments | Identifies 2–3; adjustments incomplete | Cannot identify rural-specific hazards |
| Open Range Law (MLO-3.4.2) | Explains correct response and all key law provisions | Explains response; minor law detail missing | Partially explains; key provisions missing | Cannot explain Open Range Law |
| Railroad Crossings (MLO-3.4.3) | Applies correct procedures for all crossing types at 100% | Applies correctly for most; minor error | Applies correctly for some; notable gaps | Cannot apply railroad crossing procedures |

**Academic Integrity Notice:**
Complete individually. Notes and manual may be referenced. Do not share answers. AI tool use is not permitted.

## 7. Reflection & Synthesis Plan

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Rural roads are more dangerous per mile than urban roads — higher speeds, limited visibility, unpaved surfaces, and isolated locations increase risk.
2. Idaho's Open Range Law places responsibility on drivers to watch for livestock — not on ranchers to fence them in.
3. Railroad crossings require specific procedures — never try to beat a train.
4. Slow-moving vehicles and farm equipment are common on Idaho rural roads — patience and safe passing save lives.

**Connection to Next Module:**
Module 3.5 (Urban Driving) contrasts rural driving with the challenges of city driving and freeway navigation — a very different environment with its own unique hazards.

**Self-Reflection Prompt:**
1. How does Idaho's Open Range Law change the way you will drive in rural areas?
2. What is the most important thing to remember at a railroad crossing?
3. How does rural driving require different skills than the urban driving you experience every day?
