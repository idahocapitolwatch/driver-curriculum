## 3. Pre-Work Plan

**Prior Knowledge Activation:**
- In Module 4.1, you learned how distraction impairs driving. Now think: how does anger or frustration impair driving in similar or different ways?
- Have you ever witnessed road rage? What happened and how did it affect other drivers nearby?

**Pre-Reading/Viewing:**
- Idaho Driver's Manual, section on Aggressive Driving (~6 pages)
- Guiding questions: What are five examples of aggressive driving? How can aggressive driving lead to road rage?

**Readiness Self-Check:**
1. Can you name three aggressive driving behaviors?
2. What is the difference between aggressive driving and road rage?
3. What should you do if another driver is acting aggressively toward you?
4. Is tailgating considered aggressive driving?

**Key Terms:**
1. Aggressive driving
2. Road rage
3. Tailgating
4. Weaving
5. Conflict reduction
6. De-escalation
7. Trigger
8. Emotional regulation
9. Courtesy

## 5. Activity Plan

### Activity 1: Aggressive Behavior Classification

**Type:** Practice
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-4.2.1
**Estimated Time:** 10 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Review a list of 15 driving behaviors.
2. Classify each as "aggressive," "potentially aggressive," or "not aggressive."
3. For each aggressive behavior, explain how it increases crash risk.
4. Check your answers.

**Deliverable:** Completed classification with risk explanations

### Activity 2: Conflict Reduction Scenario Analysis

**Type:** Application
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Content
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-4.2.2
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** No (formative)

**Instructions:**
1. Read 6 scenarios involving aggressive drivers (tailgating, cutting off, honking, gesturing, weaving, blocking).
2. For each, select the best conflict reduction strategy and explain why.
3. Describe what could happen if you responded aggressively instead.
4. Compare to model answers.

**Deliverable:** Written strategy selections with explanations

### Activity 3: Aggression Awareness Discussion

**Type:** Interaction
**Interaction Mode:** Learner-Learner
**MLO(s) Addressed:** MLO-4.2.1, MLO-4.2.3
**Estimated Time:** 15 minutes
**Graded:** Yes (5% of module grade)

**Instructions:**
1. Share an observation of aggressive driving you have witnessed (as a passenger, pedestrian, or bystander — do not describe illegal behavior you participated in).
2. Identify which aggressive behaviors were present and where on the escalation continuum the situation fell.
3. Describe what the driver could have done differently.
4. Respond to one classmate's post with your analysis.

**Deliverable:** Discussion post (minimum 100 words) + 1 response (minimum 30 words)

## 6. Assessment Plan

### Assessment: Module 4.2 Stop and Check Quiz

**Type:** Quiz
**MLO(s) Assessed:** MLO-4.2.1, MLO-4.2.2, MLO-4.2.3
**Weight:** 10% of module grade
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Attempts:** 2 allowed

**Description:**
Quiz covering aggressive behavior identification, conflict reduction strategies, escalation continuum, and Idaho penalties.

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Behavior Classification (MLO-4.2.1) | Classifies 90%+ of behaviors correctly with clear risk explanations | Classifies 80–89% correctly | Classifies 60–79% correctly | Classifies fewer than 60% correctly |
| Strategy Selection (MLO-4.2.2) | Selects and justifies correct strategies for all scenarios | Selects correctly for most; minor justification gap | Selects for some; reasoning vague | Cannot select appropriate strategies |
| Escalation Understanding (MLO-4.2.3) | Explains full continuum with consequences at each stage | Explains most stages; minor gap | Explains some stages; consequences missing | Cannot explain escalation continuum |

**Academic Integrity Notice:**
Complete individually. Notes and manual may be referenced. Do not share answers. AI tool use is not permitted.

## 7. Reflection & Synthesis Plan

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Aggressive driving behaviors (tailgating, speeding, weaving) significantly increase crash risk for everyone on the road.
2. Emotions and situational stress are the primary triggers — recognizing your emotional state before driving is a critical safety skill.
3. The escalation from aggressive driving to road rage follows a predictable pattern — you can break the cycle at any point by choosing not to respond.
4. The best strategy is prevention: allow extra travel time, practice courtesy, and never take other drivers' actions personally.

**Connection to Next Module:**
Module 4.3 (Impaired Driving) covers how alcohol and drugs impair the same driving abilities that aggression compromises — judgment, reaction time, and risk assessment.

**Self-Reflection Prompt:**
1. What situations are most likely to make you feel frustrated while driving? How will you manage those feelings?
2. Why is it important to never engage with an aggressive driver, even if they are clearly wrong?
3. How can being a courteous driver reduce aggression in other drivers around you?
